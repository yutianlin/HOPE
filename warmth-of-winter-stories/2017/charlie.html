Charlie

“If I had a twin brother, and if we shared the exact same genes and everything, that doesn’t mean we will share the same experiences”

“I’ve made my choices, but if others are not giving me much to choose from to begin with, is it my fault that I’ve ended up where I am today?”

Matthew’s Reflection

He came from Victoria and has spent the last two years without a home. He is entangled with drug addiction, and arrived at Vancouver three months ago. I asked him what he thought of the big city, he said there are both ups and downs, that it was an international city, but the situation with people living on the streets is amplified by the scale and size of Vancouver.

“I see these empty buildings being brought up”, he showed immense criticism towards the situation with property and treatments towards the homeless, a single shelter can take on different systems depending on the person in charge, thus it wasn't a stable place to be, it wasn't a home.

He wasn’t a man that would commit to criminal acts, not willingly by his own standards, but how long can a person stay to their ways until they break out? When they are desperately hungry, in need for clothes to prepare for the cold weather, how long would it take for them to “cross the line”?

He said 6-9 months, that was how long he lasted for before having to step out of his comfort zone, to take actions for his survival. I asked him what he thinks is the “first step” to solve all the issues he is facing, he said “a place to live, a place where I can safely store my things”. To travel and move from place to place he needed a passport, however it was stolen. He was able to apply and obtain his birth certificate only to be stolen again. He is a man with no family, absent of support from a social group, he can only keep on going for so long until he feels deteriorated and becomes unmotivated. “If I apply for it again, and it gets stolen, then what’s the point?”

I asked about jobs opportunities, would getting a job help, he said yes but that isn’t the first step, nor is it anywhere close.

“We aren’t even allowed in shops to buy food even if we had money because of the way we look. We can’t buy new clothes either, so how will we be fit for work if we cannot have a healthy diet?”

It is a vicious cycle he is living through, which leads back to his point, are all these really his fault? If he steals or commits an offence, it is because he hasn’t been given much of a choice, he is doing whatever he can to survive and as he said, he believes he can only remain sane and stick to his moral guidelines for so long until being consumed by this lifestyle.

With each attempt he took to change his life, from applying for his birth certificate, to trying to live in shelters, all went flip side which pushed him back out into the streets. The cycle continues and it will only get worst if there is no one to stop it, someone to make the homeless feel secured, protected and to not feel vulnerable by giving them a place to live, to be treated well, fed well. They are humans too, just as capable as any other, they just need more of a push, a head start to rear them out from where they are right now, and to be able to sustain themselves after all the hardships, the scarring they have gone through.

Lastly, I asked him of his childhood dreams, “When I was a kid, we were brought to the science museum and I got to ride in this underwater capsule that simulated travelling in the ocean.

I want to travel.” I think it is important for him to remember this, a distant image able to rear him back to realisation that there is something to look forward to. However, if the case is bad, this image would only remind him of what seems to be a cemented, recurring cycle, and that is an absolute tragedy. He should not be reminded of his current fate, but how it could change. He should have the right to make up for his decisions, and not penalised by the poor outlook of other people.

His name is Charlie.

Vanya’s Reflection

When you walk through downtown you tend to be fully focused on wherever you’re rushing to, or whoever you’re with. Warmth of Winter gives you the chance to take the time to focus on what, and who, is around you.

As my partner Matthew and I left waterfront station, we were somewhat hesitant on approaching residents of the Downtown Eastside Community. It’s not everyday that you initiate conversations with complete strangers.

As we walked towards Hastings however, we saw a man sitting with a bag and a box of cereal. He seemed like he wasn’t busy and I figured, I may as well gather my courage and just try to go up to him, since we had to talk to someone eventually. I’m not the most outgoing person out there, but I decided to, essentially, yolo it and try my luck at being more extroverted.

We approached the man, who was named Charlie, and it was a lot easy to strike up a conversation than I expected. We sat with him for about an hour, and I didn’t even notice the time going by. The only reason we ended up leaving was because we didn’t have much longer to give out the rest of our care packages.

Charlie had a lot of ideas about how Vancouver could try and improve the homelessness situation. He had dreams of taking a road trip. He struggled to replace his stolen passport. The conversation flowed as it would between debating friends, which is honestly not something that I expected. He informed me about things I didn’t even know were happening in the community, and I got the opportunity to see a completely different perspective on life.

The biggest thing, though, that I got out of the exchange was how easy it can be to talk to someone. All you have to do is take the first step and reach out.
